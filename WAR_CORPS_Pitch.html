INTERNALLY DISPLACED — 6.9 MILLION

The majority fled eastward-facing oblasts — Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson — toward western cities like Lviv, which absorbed 100,000 arrivals per day in the opening weeks.

600,000 fled Kyiv alone — not from bombs, but from blackouts. Russia destroyed 70% of Ukraine's power generation. Millions were left with 3–4 hours of electricity daily. DTEK said the capital lost 600,000 residents to energy deprivation by Jan 2026. They dispersed to Lviv, western Ukraine, and abroad.
Czech Republic hosts the most refugees per capita in the world — 36 per 1,000 residents. Poland is second at 26.4 per 1,000.

"WELL, IT'S CERTAINLY BETTER THAN WAR CORPS."

In 1961, JFK asked Americans to serve. 200,000 answered over 65 years. Two of them — Jeff Hartman and Ed Ma — served in Ukraine. They learned the language, the land, the people. They went home. Then Russia invaded, and they went back.

Jeff founded Ukrainian Action — 500 trucks driven from London to the front. Ed founded Ed's Friends Ukraine — delivering ultrasound equipment, ambulances, and surgical supplies from Texas to forward stabilization points where medics use them to detect pneumothorax, internal bleeding, and shrapnel wounds in real time. Ed received the White House President's Volunteer Service Award, signed by President Biden.

They are not soldiers. They are the Peace Corps generation who went to war — not with weapons, but with trucks, medicine, and an unshakable refusal to look away. They are the War Corps. And this is their film.

WHY THIS FILM.
WHY NOW.

Every day, millions of hours of footage pour out of Ukraine. Drone strikes. Trench warfare. Civilians running. But it arrives as an endless scroll — decontextualized, dehumanized, disposable.

What nobody films is the invisible infrastructure that keeps the country alive. NGOs are the backbone of modern warfare. They deliver the water, the medicine, the trucks, the ambulances, the surgical equipment. Without them, the civilian population collapses. Governments send weapons. Volunteers keep people breathing.

This film shows both. Names. Faces. Stakes. Real people through real-time diaries — the fighters, the volunteers, and the civilians living inside the most consequential conflict of our generation.

The footage is exclusive. The access is unprecedented. The story is still unfolding.

DAY 2 — THE LONG ROAD

The novelty wears off. The mission becomes miles, fatigue, logistics, bad coffee, weather, jokes, and discipline. Early morning tired faces. Gas station life. Truck interiors. Drivers eating on the move. Quiet cab thoughts. The unglamorous reality of the trip.

DAY 3 — BORDER / TRANSITION / TENSION

The journey shifts from road trip to war-adjacent mission. The mood changes. People get quieter. Hands on steering wheels. Documents. Waiting. Jeff focused. Someone says "this feels real now." No sensitive border filming. No route screens. No location details.

THE DRIVER.
A STORY WITHIN THE STORY.

The double-amputee driver is one of the emotional centers of the film — not because of disability, but because of agency, humor, grit, independence, and transformation. He is not an "inspiration object." He is a complete person. The strongest version of this story is not "look what he can do despite disability." The stronger version is: "This person chose to put his body, time, humor, will, and heart into the mission — and the journey changed him."

CONSENT RULE: Before filming anything personal, ask: "Are you okay if we film this?" Especially before filming: putting on or taking off prosthetic legs, residual limbs, pain, private morning routine, getting into/out of the truck, driving adaptations, moments of frustration or physical struggle. If he says no, do not film.

MORNING ROUTINE (with permission)

Film horizontal. Don't make it slow, sad, or dramatic. Let it be practical, real, and maybe funny. Alarm. Prosthetic legs beside the bed. Hands adjusting socks, liners, straps. Putting on legs. Standing up. First steps. Walking to the truck. Getting in. Hands on steering wheel. Engine start.

"What is the part of the morning routine nobody sees?"

DRIVING SHOTS (do not distract)

Hands on wheel. Road through windshield. Mirror reflection. Face concentrating. Laughing with passenger. Adjusting position at fuel stops. Getting out to stretch. Other drivers checking in naturally.

"What does your body have to manage on a long drive that other people don't realize?"
